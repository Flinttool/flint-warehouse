Step 1 — Cost of a Non-Functioning System (Annual)
When a system is running but not conditioning air, homeowners incur real daily costs. We estimate a conservative floor of $900/year — approximately $75/month — covering temporary cooling or heating solutions, portable unit rentals, service call fees, and daily comfort disruption. This figure is intentionally conservative. Actual costs are typically higher depending on the number of people in the home, local temperatures, and whether hotel stays or extended rentals are involved. If your situation involves more than basic inconvenience, your real cost-of-waiting is likely several times this estimate.
Operating Cost = $75/month × 12 = $900/year (applied only when system is not operating properly)
Cited Fact
Step 2 — Age-Related Efficiency Loss
Based on EPA and DOE HVAC performance data, systems lose approximately 3–5% efficiency per year after year 8. We use a conservative 3.5% figure, capped at 40% total loss. Applied to the HVAC portion of your energy bill (estimated at 45% of total bill, per DOE residential energy data), scaled by home size and unit count, multiplied by 12 months.
Efficiency Loss = (Age − 8) × 3.5% × sizeMultiplier — capped at 40% Annual Waste = Monthly Bill × 45% × Efficiency Loss × unitCount × 12

Step 5 — Estimated Annual Savings
Based on ENERGY STAR replacement data, a new high-efficiency system typically reduces energy consumption by 25–35%. We use a conservative 30% savings rate. Energy savings estimates are adjusted for your selected climate region based on average annual runtime differences across U.S. climate zones — sourced from EIA residential energy consumption survey data. Hot and arid climates see higher savings potential due to extended cooling seasons; mild climates see lower savings due to reduced runtime.
Annual Savings = Monthly Bill × 30% × sizeMultiplier × unitCount × 12 × regionMultiplier
Industry Standard
Step 6 — The Rule of $5,000
The Rule of $5,000 is a real heuristic used by HVAC professionals to evaluate repair vs. replace decisions. Multiply your system age by any repair quote. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is generally considered the more financially sound choice. This is not a guarantee — it is a widely used decision framework, not a precise financial calculation.
Rule of $5,000 = System Age × Repair Quote Example: 14 yrs × $450 = $6,300 → Exceeds $5,000 → Replacement worth considering
