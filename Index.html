Step 1 — IECC Climate Zone Detection
Your city is mapped to an IECC (International Energy Conservation Code) climate zone — the same framework used by HVAC manufacturers, energy codes, and utility companies nationally. Eight zones based on heating and cooling degree-days. Zone 1 is South Florida (maximum cooling intensity). Zone 8 is Alaska. Your zone directly adjusts age thresholds, efficiency loss rates, repair cost multipliers, signal weights, and savings potential — because the same system in Phoenix and Minneapolis faces fundamentally different wear conditions.
Zone 4 (Mixed/Humid) — National average baseline

Step 3 — Zone-Adjusted Efficiency Loss & Age Thresholds
Based on EPA and DOE HVAC performance data, systems lose approximately 3–5% efficiency per year after year 8. The exact rate and age thresholds vary by IECC zone. Hot/humid zones (1–2) see higher rates and flag systems earlier. Cold zones (6–7) have lower rates and longer useful lives. The age threshold triggering replacement urgency shifts from 12 years (Zone 1) to 20 years (Zone 8).
Zone 4: 3.5% efficiency loss/year after year 8. Replace threshold: age 15.

Step 4 — Zone-Adjusted Repair Costs & Signal Weights
Base repair cost per event: $400 (HomeAdvisor/Angi average), adjusted by a zone repair multiplier. Hot/humid zones carry higher multipliers due to increased wear frequency. Performance signals are also zone-weighted — "system running constantly" is more urgent in hot zones (cooling failure in extreme heat); "uneven temperatures" is more urgent in cold zones (heating distribution failure in winter).
Zone 4 repair multiplier: 1.00×. Signal multiplier: 1.00×.

Step 5 — Zone-Adjusted Savings Estimate
Based on ENERGY STAR replacement data, a new high-efficiency system reduces energy use by 25–35%. We use a conservative 30% baseline adjusted by a zone savings multiplier from EIA residential energy consumption survey data. Zone 1–2 homeowners see the highest returns (systems run the most hours). Zone 7–8 see lower overall savings multipliers but higher heating efficiency gains.
Savings = Bill × 30% × sizeMult × unitCount × 12 × zoneSavingsMult
Industry Standard
Step 6 — The Rule of $5,000
A real heuristic used by HVAC professionals: multiply your system's age by any repair quote. If the result exceeds $5,000, replacement is generally the more financially sound choice. Widely used in the industry — not a guarantee, but a reliable decision framework.
Rule of $5,000 = System Age (years) × Repair Quote → exceeds $5,000 → replacement favored
